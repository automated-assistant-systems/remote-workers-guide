# Sources and Further Reading

The ideas discussed throughout this book draw on several influential works related to technology, productivity, and economic change.

**Technology and the American Economy (1964)**  
U.S. National Commission on Technology, Automation, and Economic Progress.  
A landmark government report examining the potential economic impact of automation and computers during the early computer era.

**The Future of Employment (2013)**  
Carl Benedikt Frey and Michael Osborne, Oxford University.  
A widely discussed study estimating the proportion of jobs potentially susceptible to computerization and automation.

**The Mythical Man-Month** — Fred Brooks (1975)  
A classic work in software engineering and project management. Brooks explains how coordination complexity increases as teams grow and why adding people to a late project often makes it later. The concept of communication overhead remains highly relevant in modern remote and distributed work environments.

**Unlimited Wealth** — Paul Zane Pilzer (1990)  
An economic perspective on how technological innovation expands productivity and creates new forms of wealth. Pilzer argues that technology-driven economies are dynamic, generating new opportunities rather than simply redistributing existing resources.
