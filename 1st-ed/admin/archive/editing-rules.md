# Editing Rules

*Editorial Standards for The Remote Worker’s Survival Guide in the AI Age*

## Editorial Objective

The editor's role is to improve clarity, flow, and readability while preserving the author's voice and intent.

Editing should make the writing **clearer and stronger**, not fundamentally different.

---

## Rule 1 — Preserve the Author's Voice

Maintain the tone defined in the style guide:

* practical
* calm
* professional
* encouraging

Do not rewrite sections in a generic AI voice.

---

## Rule 2 — Reduce Wordiness

Prefer concise sentences.

Example:

Before
"At this point in time many professionals are beginning to recognize the potential benefits that AI tools may be able to provide."

After
"Many professionals are beginning to recognize the benefits of AI tools."

---

## Rule 3 — Remove Repetition

If a concept has already been explained clearly, avoid repeating it unless the repetition serves a teaching purpose.

Common repetition patterns to reduce:

* repeated explanations of what AI is
* repeated warnings about job loss
* repeated tool introductions

---

## Rule 4 — Strengthen Transitions

Sections should flow logically.

Weak transition:

"AI tools are powerful. Remote work has also grown."

Improved transition:

"As remote work has expanded, AI tools have become an important way to manage the growing complexity of distributed work."

---

## Rule 5 — Prefer Concrete Language

Replace vague wording.

Before
"AI can help you improve your workflow."

After
"AI can summarize meetings, draft emails, and organize research notes."

---

## Rule 6 — Maintain Consistent Structure

Most sections should follow this general structure:

1. Concept explanation
2. Example or case study
3. Practical workflow
4. Tools that enable the workflow
5. Key takeaway

---

## Rule 7 — Avoid Marketing Language

This is a practical guide, not a sales document.

Avoid phrases like:

* "revolutionary"
* "game-changing"
* "groundbreaking"

Unless supported by evidence.

---

## Rule 8 — Maintain Reader Accessibility

The target reader is not necessarily technical.

When introducing tools or concepts:

* explain them briefly
* avoid assuming prior knowledge

---

## Rule 9 — Flag Weak Logic

Editors should identify:

* unsupported claims
* unclear reasoning
* exaggerated conclusions

These should be flagged for fact-checking.

---

## Rule 10 — Protect Authorial Intent

Do not:

* change the argument of a section
* remove useful examples
* simplify ideas to the point of losing meaning

When in doubt, suggest improvements rather than rewriting the entire section.
