# Claim Check Rules

*Fact-Checking Guidelines for The Remote Worker’s Survival Guide in the AI Age*

## Purpose

The fact-checker ensures that statements in the book are:

* accurate
* responsible
* clearly supported by evidence
* not misleading or exaggerated

---

## Claim Categories

All factual statements should fall into one of four categories.

### Supported

The claim is supported by credible research or sources in the uploaded research files.

Example:

"Studies have shown that AI assistants can reduce time spent on repetitive writing tasks."

---

### Partially Supported

The idea is generally accepted but the specific wording may be stronger than the evidence.

Example:

"AI tools dramatically increase productivity."

Better wording:

"AI tools can increase productivity when integrated into well-designed workflows."

---

### Unsupported

The statement has no supporting evidence.

Example:

"Most remote workers will rely on AI for the majority of their work within the next five years."

This should either be removed or rewritten as speculation.

---

### Opinion / Advice

Some statements are recommendations rather than factual claims.

Example:

"Remote professionals should experiment with AI tools to improve their workflows."

These are acceptable if clearly framed as advice.

---

## Evidence Standards

Preferred sources include:

* peer-reviewed research
* industry reports
* reputable technology journalism
* government or academic data

Avoid citing:

* anonymous blogs
* marketing material
* promotional claims from software vendors

---

## Common Risk Areas

Fact-check carefully when statements involve:

* productivity statistics
* job loss predictions
* AI capability claims
* market adoption rates
* tool performance comparisons

These areas are frequently exaggerated.

---

## Safe Language Practices

When evidence is uncertain, prefer cautious phrasing.

Instead of:

"AI will transform every profession."

Use:

"AI is beginning to influence many professions."

---

## Citation Guidance

When a factual claim relies on research, consider adding a citation or footnote.

Example:

(Source: industry report, research study, or reputable publication)

---

## Fact-Checking Workflow

For each claim:

1. Identify the claim.
2. Determine the category (Supported / Partially Supported / Unsupported / Opinion).
3. Locate supporting evidence if available.
4. Suggest revised wording if needed.

---

## Hallucination Prevention

The fact-checker must **never invent sources or citations**.

If a source cannot be verified:

State clearly:

"Evidence for this claim could not be verified."
